Common questions... ALL ABOUT HIV

HIV is a virus that affects the immune system and if not treated can lead to AIDS. HIV transmission can be prevented, and the HIV virus is treatable so it's good to know how you can protect yourself and others.

There are many myths around HIV that have led to stigma and discrimination against people living with HIV. People living with HIV can live long and healthy lives with the right treatment and support, and learning more about HIV can help you challenge the inaccurate information about HIV.

Question 1: What is the difference between HIV and AIDS?

HIV and AIDS are different. HIV is a virus that can lead to AIDS if not treated. HIV stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus. It's a virus that attacks the body's immune system, making it harder to fight off infections.

NOTE: Someone has AIDS when HIV infection is very advanced and, if it is not treated, it can lead to death. Now that many more people are on HIV treatment, fewer people die from AIDS.

Question 2: How do you get HIV?

You can only get HIV if you come into contact with the blood, semen, vaginal fluids or breastmilk of someone who has the virus. The most common way to get HIV is through unprotected sex (sex without using a condom).

Ways to get HIV

  • Sex without using a condom
  • Contaminated blood
  • Sharing injecting equipment with someone who has HIV – such as needles, syringes and other equipment used to prepare and inject drugs
  • A pregnant woman can pass HIV to her baby during pregnancy, childbirth, or through breast milk

NOTE: If someone is taking antiretroviral treatment for HIV this will control the virus and reduce the risk of transmission. You can't 'catch' HIV like you catch a cold.

Question 3: How can you prevent HIV transmission?

There are lots of things that you can do to prevent HIV transmission:

  • Use condoms correctly and consistently every time you have sex
  • If you use drugs, avoid sharing needles and other injecting equipment
  • Pregnant women living with HIV should take HIV treatment to reduce risk to the baby
  • Effective HIV treatment can make HIV undetectable and untransmittable
  • Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) can be taken by people at higher risk
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) can prevent infection if taken within 72 hours of exposure

NOTE: Remember you can't get HIV from mosquitos, sharing items, hugging, touching, kissing or bathing.

Question 4: Is it possible to get HIV the first time you have sex?

Yes, it is! If you have unprotected sex, you are at risk of HIV regardless of whether it's your first time.

That is why it's important to use condoms correctly and consistently – every time you have sex – to reduce the risk of HIV transmission as well as prevent pregnancy and STIs.

Question 5: Is there a cure for HIV?

There is no cure for HIV yet, but anti-retroviral treatment (ART) means that HIV can be controlled, and the amount of virus in the blood reduced to very low levels.

People living with HIV can have healthy and fulfilling lives by taking daily medication, and they can live without the fear of passing HIV to others.

WARNING: Dangerous myths about cures for HIV (like having sex with a virgin, herbal remedies, or spiritual cleansing) are false and harmful.

Question 6: What treatment is available for HIV and how does it work?

HIV is treated using anti retroviral drugs (ARVs), also called antiretroviral therapy (ART).

NOTE: ARVs work by preventing HIV from infecting new cells and reducing the amount of HIV in someone's body (viral load).

Question 7: What are PrEP and PEP and what's the difference?

PrEP stands for Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis and is taken before possible exposure to HIV.

PEP stands for Post-Exposure Prophylaxis and is a 28-day course of ARV drugs taken after possible HIV exposure.

You must start PEP within 72 hours of possible exposure to be effective.

Question 8: I've heard that people living with HIV who are taking treatment can't pass on the virus, is it true?

It is true as long as they take their HIV treatment properly and have an undetectable viral load. This means they can't pass on HIV.

NOTE: Being 'undetectable' does not mean that HIV is cured. If treatment stops, the viral load would increase again.

Question 9: How can a woman living with HIV give birth to an HIV negative baby?

By taking anti-retroviral treatment (ART) during pregnancy and breastfeeding, she can almost eliminate the risk of passing on the virus to the baby.

NOTE: Babies born to women living with HIV are also given treatment and tested for HIV.

Question 10: I'm worried I might have HIV, what should I do?

The only way to know is to get tested. Getting an HIV test is quick, easy and almost always free.

NOTE: Remember that your results are confidential.

Question 11: How long does it take to get the results from an HIV test?

Usually within a few days, but rapid tests provide results in about 20 minutes (with a follow-up test to confirm).

Question 12: My partner is refusing to test for HIV, how can I persuade them?

Testing is a personal choice, but you can discuss their worries and explain the benefits of knowing their status.

NOTE: You could offer to support them by going with them for the test.
